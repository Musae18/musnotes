Discussing Certainty Without Certainty

April 19, 2026 · 2 min | 
  • ID

I once wrote in a caption that I am “more often confused than certain.” And right now, as it happens, I’m genuinely confused about what to write for my first post.

My imaginary friend gave me a suggestion.

“Just write about certainty.”

Alright then. Let’s discuss certainty without certainty.

What is truly certain in this universe?

I would probably answer that question with a faint smile and say,

“Perhaps the only certainty in the universe is our perception of matter within space and time.”

A strange answer, isn’t it?

But before I try to explain it, one thing is bugging me.

Who asked that question in the first place? Oh, right. It was me.

If the question of certainty itself is born from my own mind, perhaps the certainty I’m searching for isn’t entirely “out there” either.

It might just be a byproduct of how I perceive the world.

We often feel that a leaf is certainly green. But is it really?

…The answer is: not necessarily.

If you search on YouTube for “how is color processed by the brain?”, you’ll find educational content explaining photoreceptors in the retina. These cells convert light wavelengths into electrical signals, which the brain then interprets as a mental experience we call color.

The question is:

Why do we see 534-545 nanometers as green? Why isn’t it some other color we’ve never even imagined?

Part of our photoreceptors - specifically cone cells like the M-cone - are sensitive to that wavelength range. The brain then compares the activation patterns:

  • S-cones -> blue
  • M-cones -> green
  • L-cones -> red

If light around 540 nm enters the eye, the M-cones fire more intensely. The brain concludes: this is green.

So, color as a property of light isn’t actually a “certainty.” It is a result of neural interpretation within Homo sapiens.

Is the leaf green? The answer depends entirely on who is doing the observing.

If “green” is just a mental label, what is actually left outside of our heads?

Perhaps the universe never actually paints matter with color. There are only electromagnetic wavelengths at around 540 nanometers.

That number would still exist even if we weren’t there to see it.

Perhaps certainty ends at the numbers.

Once that number hits the retina, certainty begins to crumble, replaced by labels invented by the nervous system just so we don’t go around bumping into trees.

In that case, when I ask what is certain in the universe, am I really looking for certainty… or am I just looking for a label that feels certain?
