One night, long before I ever knew poetry, little Mus was awakened by the sound of weeping, rising and falling like a haunting scale.

A girl in an ivory-white dress sat in the corner of the room, leaning against the cold wall. Her knees were drawn tightly to her chest, her arms wrapped around them, her head bowed, and her face hidden in the fold of her arms. In that small space between breath and sob, tears fell.

Panicked, I called for my mother. But Mother came carrying only a sentence: "There is no one here, go back to sleep."

Yet, the girl was stubborn. She refused to be evicted from the crevices of my neurons. She stayed there, growing up alongside my doubts and my knowledge.

Years later, I met her again in an imaginary café—a "verbal entity" where the tables and chairs were fashioned from strings of adjectives. The girl sat alone, sipping coffee whose steam smelled of memory.

"Alone? May I join you?" I asked, as if we were merely two strangers sharing a frequency in a university hallway.

The girl smiled. "I remember you. You once entered my room without knocking," she said. "Suddenly falling asleep on my bed. I cried because I was afraid to see a stranger sleeping so soundly in my place."

I was stunned. "But that was my room," I replied softly. "I should have been the one afraid, with an uninvited guest in the corner."

We laughed then. A laughter synchronized across realities. We began to talk about trivial things: from the bathroom to the absurdities of humans in different dimensions. In that café, we agreed that what is real does not have to be touchable; it only needs to be speakable.

Until finally, I remembered a hole in my memory.

"What is your name?"

The girl fell silent. Her face grew blurred again, as if she would evaporate if not immediately given an identity. "I have no fixed name," she whispered. "I am whoever you imagine me to be."

I took a breath, drawing a name from the drawer of my knowledge.

"Leptosia," I said. "Because the white of your dress is as thin as the wings of Leptosia nina. And because when I first saw you, my mother was singing Nina Bobo to lull me back to sleep."

The girl looked down. The name fit her body like a garment newly tailored.

"Leptosia..." She tasted her own name. "That name feels as though it has been waiting at the tip of your tongue for a long time. Now I exist, Mus. Real—at least within your synapses, or perhaps in a place far beyond your world."

In that imaginary café, amidst the coffee dregs and the glow of the hanging lamps, Mus felt a circle finally close. A childhood fear had officially been renamed as an encounter.
