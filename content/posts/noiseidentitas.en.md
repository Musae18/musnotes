In my previous post, I discussed how [identity](https://musae18.github.io/musnotes/posts/identitas/) is an ever-moving biological and cognitive process that forms our assumptions about ourselves. However, the question remains: what if identity is actually a label attached to us by those on the outside?

To understand this, we can look at how identity is verified. In everyday life, identity is often reduced to data: name, place of birth, status, or occupation. It is as if, with that information, a person can be "understood."

Something similar happens in biology. To identify an organism, various indicators are required: morphology, anatomy, physiology, and even molecular data. This process is complex—so much so that taxonomy is often considered one of the "grayest" areas in biology.

Yet, there is a simple question: does the organism being identified actually care about its own complex identity?

The answer is no.

Unless, of course, that organism is the one identifying itself—like *Homo sapiens*.

At this point, the difference becomes clear. In science, identity is the result of observation. In humans, identity is also a mental experience.

This thought reminds me of a verse from a book by Sapardi Djoko Damono and Rintik Sedu:

>"siapa gerangan yang sudi mendengarkan kita bernyanyi, kecuali nyanyian itu sendiri"
> — Sapardi Djoko Damono & Rintik Sedu, *Masih Ingatkah Kau Jalan Pulang*
>(Who on earth would be willing to listen to us sing, except the song itself?)

Of course, this verse can be interpreted in many ways. But to me, the "song" represents subjective experience: the emotions, memories, and meanings that can only be fully understood by the individual.

To sing is an attempt to project that experience outward.

But, do others truly "listen"?
Or do they only *feel* like they are listening?

Perhaps, when someone receives our expression, what they understand isn't our actual experience, but their own interpretation of it—a mental model they’ve constructed based on their own perception.

If that’s the case, then understanding between individuals isn't a transfer of meaning, but a construction of meaning.

And here, the question shifts:

Can anyone truly understand us, 
or do they only understand the version of us that exists within their own minds?

{{< mus-divider >}}

The inability to truly "hear" someone else's song often leads to one thing: labeling. Labels are the easiest way for others to end the complexity of interpretation. Yet, at the same time, we often fall into that same exhaustion.

I am reminded of another part of the same book, about how a sign is destined to be an interpretation of itself. And when it tires of interpreting, it no longer cares about its own interpretation.

Aren’t we the same? A sign that never finishes interpreting itself?

The big question: if we ourselves are signs that never finish self-interpreting, how can we demand that others have the "correct" interpretation of us?

{{< mus-divider >}}

Perhaps identity is never meant to be fully understood by others. It is like *silent music*, with a frequency that resonates most perfectly only in our own ears. Others might only catch it as distorted sound, which they then name with certain labels.

Those labels remain "noise" to our subjective experience. However, like any signal interference, we might absorb some of that noise, reinterpret it, and slowly let it become part of the composition we hear as "me."
