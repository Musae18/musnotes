> In a cemetery in Vienna, on the tombstone of Ludwig Boltzmann, an equation is engraved:
>
> \begin{equation}
> S = k_{\mathrm{B}} \,\, \ln \,\, \Omega
> \end{equation}
>

This equation connects entropy ($S$) with $\Omega$, the number of possible states a system can occupy, with $k_{\mathrm{B}}$ as the Boltzmann constant. The more possibilities available, the higher the entropy.

In the realm of physics, entropy is not a moral judgment on "messiness," but rather a measurement of the number of possible configurations.

If our identity is viewed as a system, then $\Omega$ can be imagined as the versions of ourselves that haven't happened yet. Static labels work by shrinking $\Omega$, reducing countless possibilities into a single, fixed form.

When we reject such labels, we are actually reopening the space of possibility. Identity moves toward a higher entropy - not to become chaotic, but to give itself the chance to explore various configurations.

Like systems in nature, identity doesn't always find its form in a single fixed state. It expands into various possibilities first, before slowly settling into a new structure.

{{< mus-divider >}}

Imagine someone who, for years, has been given the static label of a "pesantren (Islamic boarding school) kid," with a daily life revolving around Arabic-language Islamic literature and holy verses. Within that framework, his identity seems to exist in a narrow $\Omega$, making him appear locked in a single configuration. At least, that's how it looks from the outside.

Slowly, that space begins to widen. He reads broader literature, constructs philosophical interpretations of poetry and novels, and discovers an interest in music. Then he teaches himself an instrument, connects biological facts with concepts in other sciences, and eventually loses himself in lines of programming code. One by one, new possibilities are added, expanding the configurations his identity can occupy.

From the perspective of an outsider who favors order, this change might look unfocused - perhaps even like he's going "off track." But he isn't destroying his identity. He is simply enlarging $\Omega$, allowing himself to explore more possibilities before settling into a certain form. In fact, it's possible that the initial identity was never truly "fixed." It only appeared stable because the space of possibility hadn't been opened yet.

Endless possibility isn't always comfortable. Amidst so many potential versions of ourselves, we are often driven to simplify. Labels become a sort of resting point - something to hold onto when complexity starts to feel exhausting. They act like [noise](../noiseidentitas/) that soothes, blurring the diversity of possibilities into a single, easily recognizable pattern. The social world works in a similar way: it finds it easier to understand things that are fixed, consistent, and predictable.

{{< mus-divider >}}

I previously wrote that [the brain and the world are mutually implicated.](../identitas/) The way we view ourselves is never born entirely from within. It is constantly shaped by a changing environment. If the world around us has a high $\Omega$, with possibilities constantly shifting, what happens when our self-view is frozen into a narrow possibility?

This synchrony gap is what slowly triggers a crisis. Labels feel comfortable because they reduce possibility and create local order. But at the same time, they make identity rigid in the face of an ever-changing environment. The world moves with high entropy, while we try to survive in a fixed configuration.

In such conditions, pressure becomes inevitable. Like an organism that must constantly adjust to its ecological environment, identity is also required to adapt. But adaptation is never fast or simple. It requires an unstable phase, where the old form loosens before a new configuration truly takes shape.

Because of this, it's only natural if, in the process of expanding these possibilities, we occasionally feel like a polar bear forced to adapt in the middle of a rainforest. The environment feels alien, the rhythm no longer fits, and every step is filled with uncertainty. It takes a long time for such an organism to find a new way of living.

So it is with identity. It needs time to process, allowing itself to be unstable, before finally finding a rhythm that is more in tune with a world that never stops changing.
